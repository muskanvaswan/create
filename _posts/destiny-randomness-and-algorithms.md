there are those of us that look at events unfolding without our interference as signs sent by the universe to set us on the right course. willing to allow what is “meant” to happen play out, reading between the lines to search for divine instructions to solve our most ambiguous dilemmas. I for one follow this outlook to a fault, taking my cues from every unexpected turn of events. for instance, if something urgent comes up before a planned date — i wasn’t meant to go on the date, if my flight gets delayed or cancelled — I wasn’t meant to travel just then. so on I go about my life, dutifully looking at every inconvenience as a redirection instead.

  

I fancy myself a hopeless romantic, but if I take my moon glasses off and put on my critique’s hat — then oh what laughable tactics, feeble attempts to wrestle meaning out of the meaningless — perhaps born from something deeper, an inability to truly fathom the concept of randomness and infinity, owing to the physical limitations of the brain.

  

fate and faith perhaps are an attempt to gain some sense of control over the uncontrollable. a soft illusion of participation. similar to how voting for our leaders makes us feel like a part of a process we cannot really change, choosing the cause of the uncontrollable makes us feel a little bit in control — not real control, but something that resembles it closely enough. a trust fall where the person we trust to catch us is a deity of our own making or our own choosing. thus the idea of fate became tied to that of faith, our choice in describing the maker or the source of destiny, equivalent to our choice in religious beliefs.

  

as varying as these beliefs are however, the channels of communication present limited options — thoughts and actions. from the bidirectional exchange between this deity and devotee, the more relevant one is from the nameless source of this destiny to us. whether you believe this god speaks to you in your dreams, through celestial events, through the infamous “sixth” sense you possess, or even not at all — it’s entirely subject to your own imagination. coincidence is another such channel that’s been quite popular. there is an emerging one however, that has piqued my interest — algorithms.

  

not in the obvious ways. we all know our phones are listening, we know that instagram preys on us with ads that fit us just right. that is not what i am talking about here.

  

unlike rain or a black cat crossing my path, algorithms are complex manmade coincidences — which leaves me with a new dilemma: does human manipulation negate the message of god.

  

i’m talking about that tarot card reading that says it has no hashtags. i am talking about the text message that pops up at the exact time that you were thinking of a question to which it is the answer. the concept of a message from the universe has gone digital. tell me it doesn’t sometimes feel genuinely surreal, and as much as you try to dismiss it as a coincidence, it progressively becomes harder to do so.

  

and when that same feeling begins to appear in places we know are constructed — when AI gives you an answer or says something not so generic that absolutely fits into your brain, we are a little taken aback, a little awed. some of us fight the urge to think it’s a message from the universe and some of us have completely given in.

  

but why is this urge here, even knowing that it’s caused by simply an algorithm written by another human? because it’s a black box. opaque, but not empty. it’s become a very prolific version of the fortune cookie, dressed in code instead of paper. we even have digital coin tosses and spinning wheels that let us trust our luck.

  

what’s behind it? in each case we assume it’s the butterfly effect that carries a message meant for us through loops and complex flows of logistics which are not under our control, driven by randomness and therefore still under the control of destiny, because destiny has control over everything seemingly random.

  

while this seems to settle the dilemma neatly — to comfortably fold algorithms back into destiny — there is a nuance that unsettles it again. there exists no deterministic algorithm that truly simulates randomness. it’s pseudo randomness at best, until quantum algorithms are considered too and those aren’t used in day-to-day products yet — a rabbit hole in itself, one that moves from illusory randomness to quantum possibilities. if you’re of the software or physics background you probably already understand this but the problem here is that this means algorithms cannot be trusted as under control of the destiny that creates nature, not yet anyway.

  

but wait, perhaps randomness isn’t even the point. perhaps resemblance is. maybe it’s more about probability. algorithms like your instagram run on a complex structure of probabilities, and an aggregation of these probabilities in enough steps decreases our control on the output enough, that it is as seemingly random as the growth of a branch in nature. but branches don’t really grow randomly, they grow in fractals, optimizing for catching maximum resources like sunlight and since branches are nature, in direct influence of the creator, they can be considered as benchmarks right? even the human body, the very first picture of our fate, is random in almost the same way, complex and painstakingly engineered for us but sufficiently out of our control by virtue of genes and simply the nature of it. then why must algorithms be singled out, perhaps it is only another evolved method for communication with our gods, and we should take the “this was meant for you” reels seriously.

  

This intentional unpacking and rationalization relieves the guilt of mindlessly allowing myself to be influenced by these technical coincidences. In typical conclusion, I do not think this is a binary answer, but rather a leading question — open to one’s judgement to differentiate between god’s plan and human manipulation.
