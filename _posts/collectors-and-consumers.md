*Rethinking Implicit Personality Buckets*

  
As soon as you read those two words, you immediately liked the former over the latter. There is class associated with that word. A collector makes you think of a person who appreciates culture, probably understands art deeply, and has a great sense of design. It makes you think of elegance and age. It reflects character and depth because it represents a long-lasting passion. A collector shows evidence of thoughtfulness, of a tailored taste and a personal sense of style.

  

A consumer, or a non-collector, on the other hand, puts forward a simple-minded profile in sharp contrast—a thoughtless person who lives their life almost rakishly, using and throwing away the objects and art they consume. To clarify, I speak of the persons who do not feel the pull or draw to gather and save things. In a simple mental bias, we see this person as careless, while the collector is carefully curated.

  

I too am awed by the collector. Every time I have met a niche collector, their coolness in my eyes has skyrocketed. I have heard many people wish to become collectors themselves, consciously accumulating items. One common item I see people wanting to collect is books.

As a reader myself, I have always romanticized libraries, the smell of old books, and the intimacy of the same pages being flipped by many loving hands. Yet, even with the resources, there has been a reluctance in me to buy physical copies of books since I was really young. It started with monetary constraints as a child, limiting how many books could be purchased. Since the limit could never keep up with how fast I went through books, I became an expert at finding books online to read. The only guilt I have carried through the years is that this act is unsupportive of the livelihoods of the authors, but I like to believe they would rather have their work be read than sit on shelves.

  

Now I find myself with access to enough resources to build a bookshelf of my own, to buy every book I may wish to read. And I tried; out of envy for the collector, I tried. But I realized I cannot—it just doesn’t sit right. I re-read my favorite books plenty of times, and those I happily buy and set on my bookshelf. But how can I buy a book that I have never read and disservice it with a life on a shelf in case I don’t like it?

  

I have mulled over this dilemma from time to time and, more often than not, concluded that I was just uncool—a consumer trying to be a wannabe collector. I kept coming back to this because something felt amiss with my conclusions. Latching onto the feeling of envy, I tried to understand why it was that I thought less of myself for not wanting something. Then it clicked. The feeling of envy is born out of the association of wealth with the two. A collector is an easy status symbol of the rich. It’s a sign that someone had enough income to spare for non-essential items and enough to source rarer ones. It shows they could spare time to gather interest in the things they collect.

  

But it doesn’t end there. Collectors are cool because not only do they have wealth, but they have generational wealth. They have had consistently extra income to maintain and invest in long-term hobbies and interests. So it’s not even the quantity, but rather the duration of their state of wealthiness.

  

If you consider this, then their coolness just goes down significantly. If it was just wealth that made them cool, then we could have assigned them respect for potentially having earned it. However, generational wealth becomes more a point of luck and plays more so into capitalism. The consumer just may not have had the resources to obtain and hoard the object of their interest. They may just prioritize more practical things, for good reasons. This creates sympathy for the consumer, but still, to a person of creativity, they maintain their position as boring in personality.

  

This reminds me of another category pair in design—minimalism and maximalism. While minimalism gained popularity in the millennial markets, the creatives of the world have started to de-influence us to see it for what it truly is at its core: an efficiency play and the direct product of a capitalistic mindset. At its roots, capitalistic minimalism is an aesthetic reserved for the wealthy; it requires immense resources to own almost nothing, because you must have the financial security to instantly replace or rent items the moment you need them. It optimizes physical space for frictionless, corporate efficiency under the guise of enlightenment. Maximalism, by contrast, has taken an uptick in tasteful design and has started to breathe life back into homes with color and whimsical touches.

  

Drawing similarities between these two opposing pairs is easy. The minimalist and the consumer both optimize resources, are averse to hoarding, and generally seem less stylistic. The collector and the maximalist have a mature sense of design, an air of opulence and grandeur, and they seemingly have more depth.

  

But don’t be so easily convinced; there are dissimilarities that are yet to be drawn out. Both consumerism and minimalism might promote efficiency, but the consumer still does consume art and interests, so they cannot subscribe to the idea of “less” in the way that minimalism fundamentally does. The consumer ingests, for the purpose of a sound comparison, the same amount of art and design as the collector; they just don’t maintain any record or evidence of it. The consumer then has something that the minimalist does not: freedom.

  

The consumer is not fundamentally frugal; they might, in fact, be sustainable. As a sustainable consumer, this individual acts as a more conscious human who values the object of their desires so much that the idea of restricting it to a single household or letting it gather dust is appalling. They utilize public libraries, digital spaces, and shared economies, choosing to let art flow through them and back into the world rather than damming it up as private property.

  

Now we can see an unexpected similarity between the minimalist and the collector—they both are subscribers to capitalism, obsessed with the physical management of objects as indicators of social standing. Now, whether you believe that capitalism is the enemy of cultural design or not is a different debate altogether; after all, being wealthy doesn’t mean you can’t have exceptional taste, and taste cannot always be inherited. But it certainly makes the collector less woke, silently chafing at their coolness. If we narrow the filter down to the intentional collectors—the ones who seek to find objects and pieces of design simply for the sake of collecting them—then the glamour fades further. They become even more pretentious, the images invert, and they become thoughtless and desperate seekers of being liked.

  

The unintentional collectors still redeem their respect, though. When they collect just by virtue of consuming consciously, they preserve an authenticity as well as the grandeur associated with them. Like a person who loves building Legos has a Lego collection because they shared many sessions of love and interest, with more value to the journey than the object. Like a person collecting cassettes to preserve their childhood. Just like the non-collectors who do not hoard still remain mundane in their imagery, the consumers mustn’t be seen as uncultivated. 

  

In gathering these considerations, I have unmasked the collector aesthetic as heavily rooted in economic privilege and, often lacking authenticity and vindicated the consumer as potentially the truer, freer lover of art. Ultimately, We shouldn’t confuse the artifacts of passion with passion itself.
